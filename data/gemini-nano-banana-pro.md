Nano Banana Pro: Professional Visual Production

Nano Banana Pro (Gemini 3 Flash Image) is built for professional-grade assets. Unlike standard image generators, it uses "Spatial Thinking" to understand how objects relate to each other in a 3D environment. To get the best results, use the 5-Ingredient Formula.

The 5-Ingredient Formula
1. Subject (Precision over Labels)
Avoid generic terms like "a man" or "a car." Instead, define specific traits and character.

Example: "A stoic robot barista with glowing blue optics and a polished chrome chassis."

2. Composition (The Director's Lens)
Define the camera's relationship to the subject. Mention lens types and depth of field for a professional "look."

Example: "A low-angle shot, 35mm lens, with a shallow depth of field to blur the background."

3. Action (Dynamic Energy)
Images feel more alive when there is a sense of movement or a specific moment in time.

Example: "Mid-stride, running through a field of swaying lavender under a heavy breeze."

4. Style (Atmospheric Lighting)
This controls the mood. Specify the color grading and the specific time of day or light source.

Example: "Cinematic color grading, muted teal tones, and warm golden hour backlighting."

5. Materiality (Tactile Textures)
Tell the model how the surfaces should feel and how light should bounce off them.

Example: "Brushed aluminum finish with soft contact shadows and realistic fingerprint smudges."

The Gold Standard for Text Rendering
Nano Banana Pro excels at typography, but it requires a specific "syntax" to avoid spelling errors or stylistic clashes.

Use Double Quotes: Always wrap the desired text in "...".

Specify the Font Style: Don't just ask for "text"; describe the weight and type (e.g., Serif, Sans-serif, Script, Bold).

Define Placement: Explicitly state where the text sits (e.g., "centered," "top-third," "wrapped around the subject").

Pro Prompt: "A glossy magazine cover with the bold text 'URBAN EXPLORER' in a white sans-serif font at the top, partially occluded by the subject's head for a layered effect."
